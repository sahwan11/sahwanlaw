Legislative Decree No. (19) of 2001 Promulgating the Civil Code

Current as of: 2026-04-20

Sahwan Law practitioner summary

The Civil Code (Legislative Decree No. 19 of 2001) is the foundational statute of private civil law in Bahrain, enacted on 1 January 2001 and commenced later that year. It replaced the 1969 Contracts Law and the 1970 Civil Wrongs Ordinance, and repealed the 1987 decree on strata and apartment ownership, consolidating the general law of persons, obligations, property, and security into a single code of over 1,000 articles. Its structure follows the pandectist tradition common to Egyptian-derived Arab codes: general theory of obligations (contract, unilateral act, unlawful act/tort, unjust enrichment and law); nominate contracts (sale, lease, agency, deposit, suretyship, compromise, etc.); real rights (ownership, including co-ownership and apartment ownership, servitudes, usufruct, mortgage, pledge, and privilege); and rules on capacity, consent, cause, and prescription. The Civil Code operates subject to any special statutes (e.g. Code of Commerce, Companies Law, Labour Law, Personal Status Law), which prevail within their scope. It is the default governing law for civil contracts entered into in Bahrain where parties have not made a valid choice of foreign law, and it supplies the background doctrine (good faith, abuse of right, force majeure, damages) applied by Bahraini courts and tribunals. No pre-cutoff amendments are on record. Current as of 2026-04-20, reflecting the pre-1 September 2025 cutoff state.
