Legislative Decree No. (14) of 1996 Promulgating the Law of Evidence in Civil and Commercial Matters

Current as of: 2026-04-20

Sahwan Law practitioner summary

The Law of Evidence in Civil and Commercial Matters (Legislative Decree No. 14 of 1996) governs how facts are proved in civil and commercial litigation before the Bahraini courts. It was carved out of the 1971 Civil and Commercial Procedures Law (whose evidence chapter it repealed) and now operates as the standalone evidentiary code. Structurally it moves from general principles (burden of proof on the creditor; burden of discharge on the debtor) through the traditional civil-law hierarchy of proof: documentary evidence (formal and customary writings, including electronic records), testimony of witnesses, presumptions, admissions, questioning of parties, the decisive and complementary oath, judicial inspection, and expert evidence. It regulates the threshold above which oral testimony is inadmissible to prove or rebut a written act, the probative value of commercial books, and the mechanics of challenging signatures, alleging forgery, and producing documents held by an opponent or by third parties. It applies across the Small, High and appellate civil and commercial courts, as well as BCDR proceedings where its Rules cross-refer. The only pre-cutoff amendment is Legislative Decree No. 28 of 2021, which updated several provisions, notably regarding electronic evidence (read together with the Electronic Communications and Transactions Law, Legislative Decree No. 54 of 2018). Current as of 2026-04-20, reflecting the pre-1 September 2025 cutoff state.
